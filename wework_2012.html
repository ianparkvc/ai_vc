üîë 5 Key Insights

01
"Space as a Service" Is Marketing, Not a Moat
Renaming something doesn't change the business model. WeWork's essence is a real estate arbitrage business ‚Äî long-term leases resold as short-term memberships. The "as a Service" framing doesn't justify a tech valuation. There's a reason Regus trades at a P/E of 15 while running the same business.
02
Duration Mismatch ‚Äî A Structural Time Bomb
10-15 year long-term lease liabilities vs. monthly cancellable membership revenue. This structure works during booms, but in a recession, it gets caught in a two-sided vise ‚Äî member attrition on one side, fixed lease obligations on the other. Structurally identical to a bank's maturity mismatch, but without deposit insurance or a lender of last resort.
03
Monopoly Test: 0.5 out of 4 ‚Äî No Network Effects
Of Thiel's four monopoly pillars (proprietary technology, network effects, economies of scale, branding), WeWork satisfies only 0.5. The absence of network effects is particularly damning. Adding members doesn't increase value for existing members ‚Äî it might even crowd the space. There are no barriers to entry either; anyone can lease a building and open a coworking space.
04
Mimetic Bubble ‚Äî Investors Mimicking Investors
SoftBank invests ‚Üí other VCs follow ‚Üí valuation rises ‚Üí "See, it went up" ‚Üí more investment. This isn't value creation ‚Äî it's a cycle of mimetic desire. WeWork's journey from $5B to $47B demonstrates that "the market can remain irrational for a long time," but not forever.
05
"A Bad Model Growing Fast" Is the Most Dangerous
WeWork's growth charts are impressive. But fixed costs grow proportionally with expansion, CapEx explodes, and cash disappears. Slow growth exposes problems early; fast growth hides them ‚Äî and when it finally blows up, it blows up bigger.
